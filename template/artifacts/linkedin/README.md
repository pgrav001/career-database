## LinkedIn-specific best practices

LinkedIn is not a resume with a different skin. Its surfaces truncate, its search is keyword-driven, and readers skim differently than a recruiter reading a PDF. What's worked:

**Headline** (220-char limit) — a hybrid of the keyword-pipe format and one clause of voice reads better than either extreme:

> `Title | Skill 1 | Skill 2 | Skill 3 | short value-proposition clause`

The pipes feed LinkedIn's recruiter search; the closing clause keeps it from sounding like every other keyword-stuffed headline. A pure keyword string maximizes search surface but reads as generic — keep it as a fallback to A/B test if recruiter response is low, not the default.

**About** — this is voice/POV space, not a resume restatement:
- The hook (first ~1–2 lines) must survive the **~270-character truncation** LinkedIn applies before "…see more." Front-load the most distinctive sentence; don't spend the opening on setup.
- Speak to the reader, short paragraphs, one strong POV. Weave proof in lightly — one or two credibility anchors, not a wall of metrics.
- Close with a soft call to action (what the user is looking for / an invitation to reach out).

**Experience entries** — each entry *also* truncates in the feed/preview, so lead with the **result**, not the setup:
- A one-line role summary + short result-led bullets scans best.
- Quantify. Complement the About rather than repeating it.

**Skills** — LinkedIn allows up to **50**; using only ~15–20 wastes a direct recruiter-search filter. To expand without padding: take 3 real target job descriptions, extract the exact keywords used to find and qualify candidates *for that role and level*, and add the ones the user can legitimately claim. Trace each addition to its source JD phrase. Deliberately *omit* keywords that carry the wrong association for the target (e.g. a term the target role's own hiring language flags as an anti-pattern), and leave headroom under 50 rather than maxing out with filler.

**Recommendations** — a standing, low-cost win. Candidates are people who've already given a strong verbal or written quote (mine `voice/peer-quotes.md`); a short, specific ask converts fast. A recommendation from someone the user *coached* ("made me a better manager") is a distinct proof angle from a peer/manager rec — worth seeking one of each.

**Intentional cross-artifact divergence** — LinkedIn is broadly public; a targeted resume is not. It's legitimate for the two to differ *on purpose* — e.g. anonymizing an internal job level on the public profile while keeping the precise level on the resume. Record such divergences in `DECISIONS.md` as intentional so a future consistency pass doesn't "fix" them.
