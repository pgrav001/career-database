# Strengths

> _Three strengths shown here (instead of the canonical 6) to keep the example tight. In a real database, these emerge from the substance after a substance-layer brain-dump — not picked top-down. See `docs/BOOTSTRAPPING.md`._

Each strength leads with a one-line claim, then names the evidence + the peer voice that backs it. The strength file is the "headline" layer — the artifact-generation flow reads this first, then pulls proof from `evidence/` + `stories/`.

---

## 1. Make the default the right thing

I redesign the operational defaults so the friction patrons hit lines up with what we actually want them to do — rather than asking staff to advocate harder, or asking patrons to overcome the system. The interesting wins are the ones where the rule change is invisible to most users but quietly shifts behavior at scale.

**Backing evidence:**
- `[[evidence/digital-bridge-program.md]]` — opt-in defaults restructured to opt-out for the first session; completion-rate jumped from 41% to 73% in the next cohort.
- (Not shown in this example, would also pull from:) Hold-pickup default flipped from email-only to SMS+email — pickup rate within 7 days went from 64% to 89%.

**Peer voice:**
- *"When she rolled out the fine elimination, she had answers for the questions we hadn't thought of yet."* — see `[[voice/peer-quotes.md]]`, attributed to Branch Manager Theresa Knox (Maple Branch), 2022-Q4.

**How to frame for different audiences:**
- **For library-leadership roles:** lead with the fine-elimination case — that's the "I changed a rule that had been default-since-forever" beat.
- **For municipal/nonprofit roles:** lead with the Digital Bridge opt-out change — same mechanism, smaller stakes, cleaner narrative.
- **For grant-funder audiences:** lead with the *outcome* deltas (41% → 73%, 64% → 89%) and treat the mechanism as the *theory of change*.

## 2. Coalition-builder across underfunded peer institutions

I make multi-org collaborations work when previous attempts have fallen apart. The pattern: rural and small-suburban library systems have aligned needs but each has been burned by an earlier coalition that produced more meetings than outcomes. I do the boring relationship maintenance other people skip — show up to the regional director calls *before* the grant cycle, return small favors, draft the language so the coalition members don't have to.

**Backing evidence:**
- `[[evidence/imls-grant-coalition.md]]` — Pinewood Digital Equity coalition, 9 counties, $487k IMLS grant (2022), beat 31 other applicants. Third attempt in the region to land a multi-county IMLS grant; first one that didn't collapse during the application phase.

**Peer voice:**
- *"She made the 9-county coalition work. I've watched three previous attempts at multi-county collaboration fall apart. The difference was her."* — see `[[voice/peer-quotes.md]]`, attributed to Regional Library Council director Jorge Saldívar, 2023-H1.

**How to frame:**
- **For "strategic partnerships" framings:** lead with the 31-applicant beat (competition signal) + the 9-county scope (coordination signal).
- **For "operational leadership" framings:** lead with the boring-maintenance pattern (showing up to regional calls before grant season) — that's the differentiator from people who only show up when they need something.

## 3. Translates funder language ↔ frontline language

I write grant applications in the panel's voice and staff communications in the staff's voice, without losing the substance in either direction. This is the trick that makes a public-sector leader fundable *and* credible internally — they're often two different skills, and most people are stronger at one than the other.

**Backing evidence:**
- `[[evidence/imls-grant-coalition.md]]` — IMLS panel reviewer's published feedback noted *"the strongest community-need narrative in the 2022 cohort"*; concurrent internal staff survey on the same program scored 4.7/5 on "I understand why we're doing this and what my role is."

**Peer voice:**
- *"Maren can write a grant in two voices simultaneously — the IMLS panel's voice and the bookmobile driver's voice."* — see `[[voice/peer-quotes.md]]`, attributed to System Director Pat Chen, 2023-Q2.

**How to frame:**
- **For executive director / system director roles:** lead with the "two voices" peer quote — it's memorable and recruiters who don't know public-sector funding will *still* understand the shape.
- **For program officer / grant-funded role pitches:** lead with the panel-reviewer feedback verbatim — a third-party funder calling your narrative "the strongest in the cohort" is the highest-credibility version of this strength.
