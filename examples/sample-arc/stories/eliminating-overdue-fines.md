# The fine elimination — changing a position I'd argued *against* five years earlier

## What this story answers

- "Tell me about a time you changed your mind on something important."
- "Walk me through a hard call you had to make that wasn't popular at first."
- "Tell me about a time you went against a long-standing organizational default."
- "How do you handle it when a peer you respect disagrees with you?"

## Situation

Pine County had charged overdue fines since the 1960s. In 2017, when I was Adult Services Coordinator, our system director floated eliminating fines for the first time. The library board hated it; most branch managers were skeptical; *I was skeptical*. My position then was that fines were a feedback signal that helped patrons track their borrowing, and that eliminating them would normalize material loss. The proposal died.

In 2022, two years into my role as Director of Digital Services, I re-opened it. Same proposal, different conclusion. I'd changed my mind.

## Task

The board wouldn't approve a fine-elimination proposal without (a) data showing the 2017 concerns were unfounded, (b) a clear plan for what would replace fines as a behavior signal, and (c) the branch managers — most of whom shared my 2017 skepticism — actively supporting it, not just complying. My job was all three.

## Action

- **Started by being honest about the reversal.** First conversation with the board chair was *"I was on your side in 2017. Here's what changed my mind."* I named the three pieces of data that shifted my position: (1) ALA peer-system data from 2018–2021 showing return-rate stability after fine elimination in comparable rural systems; (2) our own collections data showing that long-overdue items were almost entirely from a small set of patrons with chronic-loss profiles (fines weren't preventing the loss; they were extracting cost from people who could pay); (3) the 2019 Pine County circulation drop that correlated with the introduction of automated fine-reminder emails — patrons were *not borrowing* to avoid fine risk.
- **Met every branch manager individually before the public proposal.** Six conversations over three weeks. Not selling the proposal — listening for the specific objection I'd missed. The Maple Branch manager (Theresa Knox) raised a concern I hadn't seen: kids whose parents had unpaid fines were de facto barred from borrowing. That became a load-bearing piece of the public case.
- **Designed the replacement behavior signal.** Instead of fines, we'd send a single SMS reminder at 7 days overdue and a second at 21 days; after 60 days the item became "lost" and the cost was added (recoverable if returned). No daily-accruing fines. I drafted this *before* the public proposal so the board's "but how will patrons know" question had a concrete answer pre-loaded.
- **Brought a branch manager to the board meeting.** Theresa presented the kids-barred-by-parents'-fines case. I hadn't asked her to — she volunteered after our individual conversation. That was the moment the room shifted.
- **Sequenced the rollout in two phases.** Eliminated fines on children's materials first (April 2022, low-risk politically), then all materials (October 2022). The 6-month gap let us run the return-rate data on children's materials and report back to the board before the full rollout.

## Result

- **Board approved fine elimination unanimously** in September 2022 — a body that had killed the same proposal 4–3 in 2017. _(source: board minutes, public record)_
- **Materials return rate held steady** at the 6-month mark on children's materials (April–September 2022 data) and at the 12-month mark on all materials (October 2022–October 2023). _(source: internal circulation reports — see `[[evidence/digital-bridge-program.md]]` for adjacent supporting data on the same operational-defaults theme.)_
- **Active borrower count rose 12%** in the 12 months after full rollout. _(source: same circulation reports.)_
- **Theresa Knox's quote** (`[[voice/peer-quotes.md]]`) — *"she had answers for the questions we hadn't thought of yet"* — was given unprompted at the post-rollout debrief and is the peer-validation source for strength claim #1.

## Reflection

The hardest part of this wasn't the data work or the rollout sequencing — it was the public reversal. I was on record (board minutes, 2017) opposing the previous proposal, and I'd given a *good* argument for that position at the time. The honest version of the story is that I changed my mind because the data changed *and* because the 2017 me had been overweighting "fines as feedback signal" and underweighting "fines as access barrier for low-income patrons." Both can be true; I'd gotten the weighting wrong.

The thing I'd do differently: I should have re-opened my own thinking on this in 2019 when the first ALA peer-system data dropped, not in 2022 when I was the executive who could act on it. There's a 3-year gap there where I held a position out of consistency with my 2017 self, not because I'd actually re-tested it. That's a pattern worth watching in myself.

## Variants

- **2-minute compress:** keep the 2017→2022 reversal frame + the "I was on your side in 2017" opening conversation + the unanimous board approval. Cut the branch-manager-individual-meetings detail (interesting but not the load-bearing beat in 2 min).
- **5-minute expand:** add the data-source breakdown (3 pieces of data that shifted the position), Theresa's volunteered board appearance, and the two-phase rollout sequencing. The expanded version lands the *"how do you actually run a contested change"* signal more cleanly.
- **If they push on "how did you know the data was right":** name the 4 peer systems whose return-rate data I pulled (Hamilton County OH, Multnomah County OR, Anchorage Public, Salt Lake County) and how I cross-checked against the ALA's published systematic review (2021). The point is that I didn't just rely on advocate-sourced data — I pulled the peer-system data myself.
- **Honest-about-weakness move (always include):** the "I should have re-opened my thinking in 2019, not 2022" beat from the Reflection. Without it, this is a hero-arc story; with it, it's a *I-learned-something-about-myself* story. Most candidates skip this beat and the story reads as overly clean.
