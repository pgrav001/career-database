# Digital Bridge — adult digital-literacy curriculum across 7 branches

## Situation

In 2018 the system director asked me to design a digital-literacy program for adults age 55+ that could run system-wide without dedicated staff at each branch. The previous attempt (2015) had run only at the two largest branches and folded after a year because it depended on a part-time instructor who left. The brief: same audience, but make it survive the next staff turnover.

## What I did

- Designed a 6-session curriculum (email, search, video calling, online banking safety, digital paperwork, mobile vs. desktop basics) that could be delivered by *any* trained library staff member — not a specialist. Built the facilitator guides + the participant workbooks myself.
- Trained 14 staff across the 7 branches (2 per branch) over a 6-week period. Audited 8 of the first sessions and revised the facilitator guide based on what tripped staff up.
- Restructured the registration flow after the 2020 cohort. The 2018–2020 default was "register for each individual session" — participants would attend the first session and silently drop off. For the 2021 cohort I flipped to "register once for the full series, opt out per session if you can't make it." Completion rate jumped from 41% → 73%.
- Secured a partnership with the regional Area Agency on Aging (2020) for outreach — they put Digital Bridge flyers in every meal-delivery route. Enrollment doubled in the next cohort.

## Quantified outcome

- **Unique participants 2018–2022:** **2,800+** _(source: program registration database, confirmed)_
- **Completion rate (post-2020 redesign):** **73%** in the 2021 cohort, **71%** in the 2022 cohort _(source: same database — completion = attended ≥5 of 6 sessions)_
- **Participants from households without home broadband:** **62%** _(source: voluntary intake survey, 2022 — self-reported)_
- **Branch coverage:** **7 of 7** branches running independently by 2022 _(source: confirmed via program calendar)_

## Supporting detail

What made this hard: the curriculum had to work for participants who'd never used a mouse *and* for participants who already had email but couldn't safely manage two-factor auth. Designing for both ends without bifurcating into two programs took 3 rounds of facilitator-guide revision. The 2020 redesign (opt-in → opt-out) was the most important call I made and the one I almost didn't make — the previous coordinator had pushed back against it for two years on the basis that "people should be opting in to learning." I ran a small A/B with two branches in 2020-Q4 before recommending the system-wide change.

The program is still running as of 2024-12 under my successor; she's added a 7th session on AI assistants which is well outside what I'd have written but is the right call for the 2024 audience.

## Frames

How to position this evidence depending on audience / role / prompt:

- **For library-leadership roles:** lead with the *survives-staff-turnover* angle (the 2015 program failed because it depended on one person; this one ran independently across 7 branches with rotating facilitators). Don't lead with the participant number — public-sector audiences will care about durability over volume.
- **For "designed for scale" framings (cross-sector roles):** lead with the opt-in → opt-out redesign and the 32-point completion-rate delta. That's the cleanest "I made a defaults change that mattered" story in the portfolio.
- **For grant-funder audiences:** lead with the 62% no-home-broadband figure — that's the equity signal funders need to justify continued investment.
