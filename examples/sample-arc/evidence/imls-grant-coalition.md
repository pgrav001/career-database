# Pinewood Digital Equity coalition — IMLS Laura Bush 21st Century Librarian grant

## Situation

The Institute of Museum and Library Services (IMLS) opened the 2022 Laura Bush 21st Century Librarian funding cycle in November 2021. Pine County had applied solo twice before (2017, 2019) and lost both times — the IMLS panel feedback both times was the same: *"compelling individual case but insufficient regional scope."* I'd been Director of Digital Services for 4 months when the 2022 cycle opened. The system director (Pat Chen) gave me the call: try again solo or build a coalition.

Three prior multi-county attempts in our region had collapsed during the application phase — the coordinating system would be 6 weeks from deadline and key partners would pull out over scope disagreements. There was a real cultural belief in our regional director circle that "multi-county doesn't work here."

## What I did

- **Started 5 months before the deadline, not 6 weeks.** The standard cycle was a frantic 6-week application sprint. I scheduled informal coffee meetings with the directors of 11 neighboring county systems in November–January, before the grant was even announced. No agenda — just listening to what each system needed and what they'd been burned by in previous coalition attempts.
- **Drafted a coalition framework that left scope at the county level.** The previous attempts had failed on "whose program is whose." I structured the application so each county owned its own programming (different across systems), with the grant funding a shared infrastructure layer (digital lit curriculum, hotspot lending policy, evaluation framework) that everyone could draw from. No system had to give up control of its own program design.
- **Wrote the grant in two voices.** The narrative section (panel reviewers) emphasized the regional inequity data — 38% of households in the 9 counties without home broadband (US Census ACS, 2021) — and the coordinated-but-distributed model. The implementation section (panel reviewers who'd run library systems) named specific staff roles and timelines.
- **Built a 9-county sign-on coalition** of the 11 I'd met with. Two declined (one because of timing, one because of internal politics with their county board). Got formal board resolutions of support from all 9 by April 2022, 6 weeks before the deadline.
- **Submitted May 2022.** Award notification arrived October 2022. Funding began January 2023.

## Quantified outcome

- **Grant amount:** **$487,000** over 3 years _(source: IMLS award notice, 2022-10; public record)_
- **Applicant pool:** **32 applications** in our category; 7 funded _(source: IMLS published cycle summary)_
- **Coalition size:** **9 counties** spanning ~340,000 residents _(source: coalition MOU, 2022-04)_
- **Panel review excerpt** (published verbatim with the award): *"the strongest community-need narrative in the 2022 cohort"* _(source: IMLS panel feedback, public record)_
- **Coalition retention:** **9 of 9 counties** still active in the coalition at the 18-month mark (2024-Q2) _(source: quarterly coalition reports)_

## Supporting detail

What made this hard: I was 4 months into a new role and had to convince 11 county system directors who'd never seen me successfully run anything regional to bet on my coalition framework. The first three coffee meetings went badly — the directors had heard the "multi-county pitch" before. The unlock was the third meeting (Tipton County's director, Marisol Esteban), who said *"if you're going to do this differently, let each of us keep our own programs and just fund the bones."* That became the coalition framework verbatim.

The 2-voice narrative section was unusual for IMLS applications, which usually pick one register. Pat Chen (system director) initially pushed back — *"funder panels want one consistent voice, not two registers"* — and I pushed back on her pushback. The 2-voice version was what got the *"strongest community-need narrative"* call-out. Worth logging as a case where the disagreement-with-leadership was the right call.
